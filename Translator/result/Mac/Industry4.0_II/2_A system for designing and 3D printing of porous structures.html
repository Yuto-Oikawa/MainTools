A system for designing and 3D printing of porous structures

A

system for designing and 3D printing of porous structures

ABSTRACT

Porous structures exhibit superior properties compared to their non-porous counterparts, though they are challenging to design and fabricate. This paper presents a self-contained and user-friendly system that designs a porous structure by filling the spaces between a randomly generated point cloud and a closed boundary. It produces the

STL

dataset of the designed structure. The system generated

STL

datasets were used to fabricate some porous structures using a 3D printer. The fabricated structures exhibited randomly sized and distributed pores through which gasses and liquids could pass. These characteristics of the fabricated structures make them suitable for engineering and biomedical applications. © 2020 CIRP. Published by Elsevier Ltd. All rights reserved.

1.

Introduction Additive Manufacturing (AM) [1] is a valuable addition to the existing manufacturing technology. In AM, first, a solid

CAD

model of the object to be fabricated is prepared. In the next step, the outer boundary of the solid

CAD

model is triangulated, resulting in facets.

A

facet consists of the three vertices of the respective triangle and a normal vector representing the orientation of the triangle-driven plane. The facets are organized in a dataset called the

STL

dataset [2]. The

STL

dataset is then sliced to define the layer-by-layer areas where the materials should be added during the fabrication. Finally, one of the

AM

processes (e.g., laser curing, fused deposition, metal sintering, and alike) is executed to fabricate the desired object following the slicing instructions. In 3D printingࣧthe most commonly used AMࣧthe process called fused deposition is used to add materials. (The phrases “AM” and “3D printing” will be used interchangeably in the remainder of this article.) One of the remarkable aspects of 3D printing is that it can fabricate structures consisting of both complex internal-external topologies and spatially distributed heterogeneous materials [3,4]. This freedom brings challenges, too, that are referred to as shape complexity, hierarchical complexity, material complexity, and functional complexity [1].Asa result, a new research field called design for

AM

has evolved to address the abovementioned complexities [1,3,4]. However, since one of the growing application areas of 3D printing is the fabrication of porous structures having biomedical, material engineering, and esthetic significances, design for 3D printing of porous structures has become an important research issue. Numerous authors have studied the design and fabrication of porous structures using different types of geometric and non-geometric modeling approaches. 2.

System development This section presents the proposed system architecture and the system itself, respectively. First, consider the system architecture. The proposed system architecture is schematically illustrated in Fig. 2.As seen in Fig.

2,

the architecture consists of the following five modules: 1) Boundary Module, 2) Point Cloud Module, 3) Space-Filling Module, 4) Triangulation Data Module, and 5) Cross-section Analysis Module. Fig.2.ProposedSystemArchitecture.

The function of the Boundary Module is to acknowledge a given nominal shape, which is a non-porous solid structure bounded by closed walls. The pores are generated in the inner areas, as well as on the walls of the nominal shape. The Boundary Module thus stores and modifies (if necessary) the

STL

dataset of the nominal shape for the sake of computation. The function of the Point Cloud Module is to create a random point cloud or to acknowledge an externally generated point cloud. The point cloud must surround the nominal shape. In addition, the Point Cloud Module analyzes the point cloud with respect to the

STL

dataset of the nominal shape and identifies the points that are inside the nominal shape. These points are referred to as the inner points. Thus, the Boundary Module and Point Cloud Module must work in a coordinated manner and prepare the necessary inputs for the next module (Space-Filling Module). At the onset of the space-filling operations, the Space-Filling Module acknowledges the nominal shape, inner points, and points on the triangulated boundary walls of the nominal shape, as shown in Fig. 3 (a). This module then fills the space between the inner points and boundary walls, as schematically illustrated in Figs. 3(b)(d). The space-filling process consists of two main steps. The first step is called the tetrahedron formation process (Fig. 3(b)). The other step is called the polyhedron formation process (Figs. 3(c)(d)). For forming a tetrahedron, a randomly selected inner point called the pivot-point is considered. The three closest points to the pivot-point are determined. These four points form a tetrahedron. This process continues. If two or more tetrahedrons share the same point as one of their vertices, the tetrahedron formed first is considered, and others are eliminated. Thus, the process shown in Fig. 3(b) creates mutually exclusive tetrahedrons. As a result, when the processing shown in Fig. 3(b)is completed, some mutually exclusive tetrahedrons, some unused points (a subset of the inner points not participated in the tetrahedron formation process), and the triangulated boundary walls coexist. These entities are further connected to fill more spaces, as shown in Fig. 3(c) by forming some polyhedrons. When the process continues, the polyhedrons are extended up to the boundary walls. After the completion of the extension process, the triangles of the boundary walls connected to the polyhedrons are removed. At the same time, the inner triangles of the polyhedrons are removed. As a result, openings are created in the structure. Fig.3.Space-fillingProcess.

The function of the Triangulation Data Module is to organize the facets of the tetrahedrons not connected to the polyhedrons, the polyhedrons, and the boundary triangles not participated in the polyhedron formation process. As a result, this module generates the

STL

dataset of the porous structure. This dataset can be transferred to a 3D printer for fabricating the porous structure, as schematically illustrated in Fig.

2.

In addition, this dataset can be transferred to the last module, that is, the Cross-section Analysis Module. The function of the Cross-section Analysis Module is to acknowledge the

STL

dataset generated by the previous module and display the cross-section corresponding to a given height. It also calculates the area of each cross-section. If the analyzed cross-sections are not the desired ones, then the user can consider a new point cloud, as shown in Fig.

2.

Based on the abovementioned system architecture, two user-interfaces are constructed, as shown in Fig.

4.

The interfaces run on a Windows-based personal computer. The first user-interface (Fig. 4 (a)) helps a user to input the desired monomial shape (non-porous counterpart of the porous structure) and to generate (or input) a random point cloud. As far as generating a point cloud is concerned, the user-interface is equipped with a mechanism that generates a random point cloud around the nominal shape using a grid-based approach where a layer-by-layer mesh of grids is created, and some A.M.M.S. The density of the random points at each layer is controlled by a user-defined parameter called density parameter (probability). Thus, the number of grids and the probability collectively control the fineness of the pores to be fabricated. 4.

Concluding remarks This article presents a self-contained and user-friendly system that can design customized porous structures by filling the spaces offered by the intersection of a random point cloud and a closed boundary. The system can produce an

STL

dataset of the designed structure for its 3D printing and for analyzing its layer-by-layer cross-sectional areas. Some porous structures having different closed boundaries are designed and analyzed by the system to see its efficacy. Afterward, the system-generated

STL

dataset is used to fabricate the structures using a commercially available 3D printer. The printing operations of the structures do not require any supports. The cross-sectional areas of the 3D printed structures are compared with the designed ones.

A

stable cross-sectional area at each layer of the fabricated structures is observed, though the randomly sized pores are distributed all over the structure. Apart from the ease of design and fabrication, the porous structures exhibit percolation, though no formal computational arrangements are made to connect the pores. In order to gain more insight into the presented approach, the following issues can further be addressed. Apart from the grid-based point cloud generation approach, other approaches (e.g., fractal geometry) can be explored. Apart from polymeric materials, metallic materials can be used to fabricate the porous structures.
