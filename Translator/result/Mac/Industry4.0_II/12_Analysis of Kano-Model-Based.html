ABSTRACTS

The Kano model is a useful model for integrating the Voice of the Customer into the subsequent processesof product development. This paper presents a method for analyzing customers’ preferences obtained byusing the Kano model. In the Kano model, customers’ preferences are obtained by using a prescribedform to know whether or not a given product attribute is a Must-be, Attractive, One-dimensional,Indifferent, or Reverse attribute for a given product. Since the preference may vary a lot from customer tocustomer due to the demographic and psychographic factors, a quantitative aggregation process is neededto compute the information content of all customer answers and finally to decide the aforementionedstatus of each product attribute. Most of the quantitative methods developed so far deal mainly with theimprecision in the customer answers and do not address the uncertain (unknown or missing) customeranswers. In fact, a significant number of customers might not be able to submit their answers on time. Asa result, a significant number of answers remain unknown that manifests a great deal of uncertainty. Thispaper shows an approach to simulate the unknown customer answers. In addition, an approach isdeveloped to measure the information content of customer answers integrating the real and simulatedcustomer answers. This is helpful for identifying the correct status (Must-be, Attractive, One-dimensional,Indifferent, or Reverse) of each product attribute. Moreover, it is shown that the weighted average type ofquantitative analysis is not suitable for quantifying the inherent complexity of customer answers obtainedin accordance with the Kano model. © 2010 Wiley Periodicals, Inc. 1.1. The Kano Model

As mentioned before, the Kano model is helpful for integratingthe VOC into the subsequent processes of product development.

This subsection describes the elements of the Kanomodel.

The Kano model of customer satisfaction [Kano et al.,1984; Berger et al., 1993; Matzler and Hinterhuber, 1998; Jiaoand Chen, 2006; Kai, 2007] qualitatively defines the relationshipbetween product attribute and customer satisfaction andprovides five types of product attributes: (1) Must-be, (2)One-dimensional, (3) Attractive, (4) Indifferent, and (5) Reverse,as schematically illustrated in Figure 2.

Referring to Figure 2, an attribute is considered a Must-beattribute if its absence produces absolute dissatisfaction andits presence does not increase satisfaction. An attribute isconsidered a One-dimensional attribute if its fulfillment helpsincrease the satisfaction and vice versa. An attribute is consideredan Attractive attribute if it leads to a greater satisfaction,whereas it is not expected to be in the product. Anattribute is considered an Indifferent attribute if its presenceor absence does not contribute much to satisfy the customers.

An attribute is considered a Reverse attribute if its presencecauses dissatisfaction and vice versa. Thus, for the meaningfulintegration of the VOC into the subsequent processes ofproduct development, it is important to do the following:

• Keep Must-be attributes.

• Add a good number of One-dimensional and Attractive

attributes.

• Avoid Indifferent attributes as many as possible.

• Avoid Reverse attributes.

However, in addition to the aforementioned classificationof product attributes (i.e., customer needs), the Kano modelprovides a format for obtaining customer answers for eachpotential customer attribute. Table I shows an example of suchformat. As seen from Table I, a customer (respondent) has toselect one of the states out of Like, Must-be, Neutral, Livewith,and Dislike from the functional side, stating his/her levelof satisfaction, if the attribute is added to the product. Thecustomer also has to select one of the states (out of the samechoices) from the dysfunctional side, stating his/her level ofsatisfaction, if the attribute is not added to the product. The combination of functional and dysfunctional answersis then used to identify the status of the attribute in term of:(1) Must-be, (2) One-dimensional, (3) Attractive, (4) Indifferent,or (5) Reverse. 3.1. Information Content of Importance

As mentioned before, the self-stated importance is a separateentity, and it is better to process it separately. This raises aquestion: What should be done to process the information ofself-stated importance? One of the alternatives is to determinethe “information content” of the self-stated importance. Todetermine the information content as prescribed by the axiomaticdesign theory of systems, two ranges are needed [Suh,1998; Guenov and Barker, 2005]. One of the ranges is called“design range,” and the other is called “system range”. Afunction of information content then quantifies how well thedesign range fulfills the system range.

The design range is a user-defined requirement. For thecase of self-stated importance, it is a statement expressing thedesired or required level of importance (e.g., “importanceshould be at least quite important”). On the other hand, asystem range is a range that a real system offers. In this case,system range is the range derived from the truth values of thestates of importance. The schematic illustration in Figure 7shows how to calculate the aforementioned information content.

As seen from Figure 7, from the customer answers thetruth values are determined, as follows: TV(Quite Imp),TV(Imp), TV(Some Imp) = 0.033, TV(Less Imp) = 0.5, andTV(Very Imp) = 0.7. This means that the maximal truth valueis max(TV) = 0.7 and the minimal truth value is min(TV) =

0.033. This range max(TV) – min(TV) = 0.7 - 0.033 = 0.667

is the system range, for this particular case, as shown in Figure

7, too. The design range depends on the requirement that the

user sets. For the case shown in Figure 7, the user sets arequirement to be “importance is less important” (i.e., the usertries to see whether or not less important is an appropriatestate of the degree of importance for the underlying productattribute). According to the given customer answers, TV(LessImp) = 0.5. It is neither the maximal truth value (0.7) nor theminimal truth value (0.033). It is between those two values.

As such, this requirement partially fulfills the system range.

In this sense, the system range is equal to the differencebetween the best truth value and the truth value of requirement(in this case 0.7 – 0.5 = 0.2). The ratio between the systemrange and design range thus determines the degree of fulfillmentof requirement or so-called information content of adesign [Suh, 1998; Guenov and Barker, 2005]. The caseshown in Figure 7 actually underlies this aspect.

Studies have been made to develop mathematical modelsand quantifies to measure the above mentioned informationcontents [Ullah, 2005a, 2005b; Ullah, Harib, and Al-Awar,2007, Ullah, Fisher, and Alawar, 2008; Ullah and Harib,2008]. According to these studies, there is type of informationcontent called Certainty Compliance (or Certainty Entropy)(CC ∈ [0,1]) that measures the intrinsic complexity of allopinions expressed on an issue (in this case, the issue isimportance). There is another type of information contentcalled Requirement Compliance (or Requirement Entropy)(RC ∈ [0,1]) that measures the degree of fulfillment of alinguistically expressed requirement (in this case, statementslike importance should be at least quite important). The otherinformation content is called coherency measure (λ), whichaggregates the information of (CC, RC) and provides a numericalscore for making the final decision.

6. CONCLUDING REMARKS

A. A Monte Carlo simulation method is presented to knowthe Kano-model-based unknown customer answers.

B. It is shown that the self-stated importance is attributedependentand likely to provide misleading selection ifit is used in the quantitative assessment. The combinationof answers for functional and dysfunctional partsof Kano questionnaire is good enough to make a validdecision.

C. A method is developed to measure the informationcontent of an attribute. This approach is found effectivein minimizing the information content of product attributeand, thereby, determining its status in term ofAttractive, Must-be, One-dimensional, Indifferent, orReverse attribute, as prescribed by the Kano model.
